## What Is a Ponzi Scheme?

According to the SEC, a Ponzi scheme has specific characteristics:

1. **Central operator** who controls the fund
2. **Promised returns** to investors
3. **Paying early investors** with money from new investors
4. **Unsustainable** - collapses when new money stops
5. **Secrecy** about how returns are generated

## Why Bitcoin Doesn't Fit

### No Central Operator
Bitcoin has no CEO, no company, and no one controlling it. Satoshi Nakamoto disappeared in 2011. The network runs on code that anyone can verify.

### No Promised Returns
No one official is promising you'll make money. Bitcoin could go to zero tomorrow. Anyone telling you otherwise is selling you something.

### Transparent Operations
The entire Bitcoin codebase is open-source. Every transaction ever made is publicly visible. This is the opposite of Ponzi secrecy.

### Sustainable Mechanism
Bitcoin's value comes from its utility as a payment system and store of value, not from new investors paying old ones. If new buyers stopped, Bitcoin would still function.

## The Valid Criticism

The strongest version of this argument isn't really about Ponzi schemes - it's about fairness:

> Early adopters got bitcoin for almost nothing. As more people adopt it, the price rises, benefiting those who already hold it. This creates a wealth transfer that seems unfair to latecomers.

This is true, but it applies to any asset with rising prices - real estate, stocks, gold, art. The question is whether the underlying value proposition is legitimate.

## A More Accurate Criticism

Rather than "Ponzi scheme," more accurate critiques might be:
- Bitcoin is highly speculative
- Price volatility makes it risky
- Wealth concentration is concerning
- Environmental impact is significant

These are fair debates. But they're different from calling it fraud.
